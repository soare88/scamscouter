Is This PayPal Email Real? How to Tell the Difference

The fastest way to check if a PayPal email is real is to look at three things: the sender address, how you are addressed, and what the email is asking you to do.

Real PayPal emails always come from an address ending in @paypal.com — nothing else. They address you by your full name, not "Dear Customer", "Dear User" or "Dear PayPal Member". And they never ask you to verify your password, card number or bank account details by clicking a link in an email.

If any of these three things are wrong, the email is almost certainly a phishing attempt. Do not click any links. Do not call any phone numbers listed in the email. Go to paypal.com directly by typing it in your browser and check whether any notification is waiting in your account.

Most Common PayPal Scam Email Types

Account suspension or limitation emails claim your PayPal account has been suspended, limited or flagged for suspicious activity. They create urgency by saying you have 24 or 48 hours to verify your details or your account will be permanently closed. The link leads to a fake PayPal login page designed to steal your username and password.

Fake invoice emails are one of the most sophisticated PayPal scams. Fraudsters use PayPal's own invoice and money request system to send legitimate-looking payment requests. The invoice may claim you owe money for a product you never ordered, or warn that a subscription will auto-renew. A phone number is included to "cancel" — calling it connects you to a scammer who tries to steal your details or gain remote access to your device.

Payment received emails tell you that someone sent you money and you need to click a link to accept or verify the payment. In some versions, the email claims the payment is on hold until you ship a product or confirm your bank details. Always check your actual PayPal balance by logging in directly — never trust a payment based on an email alone.

Overpayment scams often target sellers. A buyer sends more than the agreed amount and asks you to refund the difference. The original payment is fraudulent and will be reversed, leaving you out of pocket for the refund you already sent.

Password reset and security alert emails claim unusual activity was detected on your account and ask you to reset your password immediately. The link leads to a fake page that captures your login credentials. A real PayPal security alert will never require you to enter your current password on an external page.

Refund and cashback emails claim you are owed a refund and need to log in to claim it. These are designed to steal your login details by creating a positive incentive rather than fear or urgency — making them slightly more effective than threat-based phishing.

8 Signs a PayPal Email is Fake

  • Sender address is not @paypal.com. Check the full email address, not just the display name. Addresses like service@paypal-support.com, noreply@paypalverify.net or info@paypal.account-alert.com are fake.
  • You are not addressed by your full name. PayPal always uses your registered full name. "Dear Customer", "Dear Account Holder" or "Hi PayPal User" are signs of a phishing email.
  • There is urgency or a deadline. Real PayPal notifications do not threaten immediate account closure or demand action within hours.
  • You are asked to verify details by clicking a link. PayPal will not ask you to confirm your password, card number, bank account or SMS code through a link in an email.
  • The link does not go to paypal.com. Hover over any link before clicking. If the destination URL is not exactly paypal.com, do not click it.
  • There is a phone number to call. Fake PayPal invoices often include a phone number to "dispute" a charge. Calling it connects you to scammers, not PayPal.
  • The email refers to a transaction you do not recognize. A payment confirmation or invoice for something you never ordered is a common trigger designed to make you act quickly without thinking.
  • Poor formatting, grammar or inconsistent branding. While some phishing emails look professional, many contain spacing errors, mismatched fonts, blurry logos or awkward phrasing.

What Real PayPal Emails Look Like

Genuine PayPal emails come only from addresses ending in @paypal.com. Common legitimate sender addresses include service@paypal.com, service@intl.paypal.com and noreply@paypal.com. The email will use your full registered name in the greeting.

Real PayPal notifications confirm actions you already took — a payment you sent, a receipt for a purchase, or a genuine account alert that appears in your PayPal notification center when you log in. If a notification in an email does not appear in your PayPal account when you log in directly, the email is fake.

PayPal also provides a direct way to report suspicious emails: forward them to spoof@paypal.com. PayPal's security team reviews these reports and can confirm whether an email is genuine.

What to Do if You Clicked a Link in a Fake PayPal Email

If you clicked a link but did not enter any information, close the browser tab and clear your cache. Run a malware check if the page asked you to download anything.

If you entered your PayPal password on a fake page, go to paypal.com directly and change your password immediately. Enable two-factor authentication from your account security settings. Check your recent transactions and linked payment methods for anything unfamiliar.

If you entered card details, contact your bank or card issuer immediately and explain that you entered your card information on a phishing page. Ask them to block the card and monitor for suspicious transactions.

Report the phishing email to spoof@paypal.com, to your country's cybercrime reporting agency, and if money was taken, to your local police. Keep all evidence including the email, links, and any confirmation screens you saw.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if a PayPal email is real or fake?

Check the sender address — it must end in @paypal.com exactly. Check that your full name is used in the greeting. And check whether the email is asking you to click a link to verify sensitive information. If any of these are wrong, it is likely a phishing email. Log in to PayPal directly to verify any notification.

What are the most common PayPal phishing email types?

Account suspension alerts, fake invoices sent through PayPal's own system, payment received notifications, overpayment scams, password reset emails and refund claim emails are the most frequently reported PayPal phishing types.

Can I check a PayPal email without clicking any links?

Yes. Copy the email text, sender address and any links into ScamScouter for an instant risk analysis. You can also forward suspicious PayPal emails directly to spoof@paypal.com without clicking anything.

What is the PayPal invoice scam?

Scammers use PayPal's real invoice system to send fraudulent payment requests. Because the email comes from a genuine PayPal address, it bypasses spam filters. The invoice claims you owe money for something you never ordered, and includes a phone number to call. Calling that number connects you to scammers, not PayPal.

What should I do if I clicked a link in a fake PayPal email?

If you entered your password, change it on paypal.com immediately and enable two-factor authentication. If you entered card details, call your bank to block the card. Report the email to spoof@paypal.com and your country's cybercrime agency.

Do real PayPal emails ask you to verify your account by clicking a link?

No. PayPal will never ask you to confirm your password, card number, bank account or SMS code through a link in an email. Any email making this request is a phishing attempt. Always go to paypal.com directly by typing it in your browser.

What does a fake PayPal sender email address look like?

Fake addresses often look like service@paypal-security.com, support@paypal-verify.net or noreply@paypal.account-alert.com. Real PayPal emails only come from addresses ending in @paypal.com with nothing after that domain.

Is the "you received a payment" PayPal email always a scam?

Not always — PayPal sends genuine payment notifications. The scam version asks you to click a link or call a number to release a payment, or claims you must ship a product first. Always verify by logging into your PayPal account directly, not through the email.
